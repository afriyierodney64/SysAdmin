MBR & GPT (Partitioning Schemes)

MBR = Master Boot Record | GPT = GUID Partition Table

Professional Definition: Architectural frameworks that dictate how a physical hard drive is divided into logical volumes (partitions) and where boot information is stored.

The Analogy: Think of a raw hard drive as an empty plot of land. Before you can build houses (save files), you need a blueprint to divide the land into lots (partitions).
- MBR is the old, handwritten blueprint. It limits you to dividing the land into only 4 big lots, and if the single piece of paper gets destroyed, you lose the layout.
- GPT is a modern, digital blueprint. You can divide the land into 128 lots, the lots can be infinitely larger, and it automatically makes backup copies of the blueprint in case the original is damaged.

RAID Architecture

RAID = Redundant Array of Independent Disks

Professional Definition: A storage virtualization technology that combines multiple physical disk drive components into one or more logical units for the purposes of data redundancy, performance improvement, or both.

The Analogy: Imagine you have a highly important, long book to write.
- If you write it in one notebook (Single Drive) and lose it, it's gone.
- RAID 1 (Mirroring): You write every page in two notebooks simultaneously. If one is lost, you have an exact copy. (High safety, but takes twice the paper).
- RAID 5 (Parity): You have 3 friends helping you. Instead of full copies, you write part of a sentence in notebook A, the next part in B, and a "math puzzle clue" in C. If notebook A burns up, you can use B and C's clues to perfectly recreate A. (Good safety, saves paper, but doing the math puzzle slows you down).
- RAID 10 (Stripe of Mirrors): You combine the speed of writing parts in different books with the safety of making exact copies of everything. (Fastest and safest, but requires the most notebooks).

Storage Models (DAS, NAS, SAN)

DAS = Direct Attached Storage | NAS = Network Attached Storage | SAN = Storage Area Network

Professional Definition: Different network architectures defining how storage devices connect to servers and workstations, determining access speed, sharing capabilities, and data control level (file-level vs. block-level).

The Analogy: You need a place to store tools (data).
- DAS is a toolbox sitting directly on your desk. It's super fast to reach, but only you can use it. (Like an external USB drive).
- NAS is a shared toolshed in the neighborhood. Anyone on the street (network) can walk up and borrow a hammer (file). The shed manager handles where the tools are placed. (Great for sharing files in an office).
- SAN is a massive, high-speed underground delivery tube system connecting your house directly to a giant warehouse. Your computer treats the warehouse exactly like a toolbox on your desk, giving you total, raw control. (Used by massive enterprise servers).

System Administration Principles & Laws

Professional Definition: Foundational philosophies that guide how IT infrastructure should be designed (Scalability, Security, Simplicity) and humorous but accurate "laws" reflecting human/system behavior.

The Breakdown:
- Scalability: A system's ability to grow without breaking or requiring a massive increase in human effort (Requires Automation).
- Simplicity: Complex systems break easily. Keep designs as simple as possible to make them understandable and fixable.
- Parkinson's Law of Storage: "Data expands to fill the space available." No matter how big a hard drive you buy, users will eventually fill it up with cat pictures and old backups.
- Murphy's Law: "Anything that can go wrong, will go wrong." Always have a backup plan, because a server will crash at the worst possible time.

SSH & Public-Key Cryptography

SSH = Secure Shell

Professional Definition: A cryptographic network protocol for operating network services securely over an unsecured network, primarily used for remote command-line login.

The Analogy: You need to send secret commands to a robot in another building.
- Password Authentication: You shout a secret password across the street. If someone is listening, they steal it.
- Public-Key SSH: You give the robot an open padlock (Public Key). Anyone can see the padlock, but it's useless on its own. You keep the only key (Private Key) safe in your pocket. When you want to talk, the robot puts a message in a box and snaps your padlock shut. Only you can open the box to read the message. The secret is never sent across the street.

Unix Philosophy: "Everything is a File"

Professional Definition: A defining characteristic of Unix-like operating systems where input/output to physical hardware devices, network sockets, and system processes is handled identically to regular text files via the Virtual File System.

The Analogy: Imagine a master control room for a factory. Normally, you need a different manual and tool to operate the lights, the conveyor belt, and the heater.
In Unix, the designers decided: "Let's put a sticky note on everything."
To turn on a light, you just write the word "ON" on its sticky note. To read the temperature, you just read the words on the heater's sticky note. Because the computer treats hardware (the heater) exactly like a text file (the sticky note), administrators can use simple text tools to control complex machinery.

Review MBR/GPT

Question 1: MBR vs GPT (20 Marks)

Compare and contrast the Master Boot Record (MBR) and GUID Partition Table (GPT) partitioning schemes. In your answer, discuss three specific architectural advantages GPT provides to modern System Administrators over the legacy MBR framework.

Tutor's Breakdown

Comparison: Both are partitioning schemes that organize how data is structured on a storage drive. However, MBR is the older, legacy standard (introduced in the 1980s), while GPT is the modern standard associated with UEFI.

Three Architectural Advantages of GPT:

  1. Storage Limits: MBR limits disk size to 2TB and supports only 4 primary partitions (requiring extended/logical partitions for more). GPT supports drives virtually infinite in size (zettabytes) and up to 128 primary partitions by default.
  2. Data Redundancy/Corruption: MBR stores partitioning and boot data in a single location at the start of the drive (making it a single point of failure). GPT stores multiple copies of this data across the disk (primary header at the start, backup header at the end), making it highly resilient to corruption.
  3. Data Verification: GPT uses Cyclic Redundancy Checks (CRC32) to constantly verify the integrity of its headers and partition tables. If it detects corruption, it automatically attempts recovery from the backup. MBR has no way of knowing if its data is corrupted.
Review RAID

Question 2: RAID Architecture (20 Marks)

A medium-sized enterprise requires a storage solution that effectively balances fault tolerance and read/write performance. Explain the operational architecture of RAID 5 and RAID 10. Recommend which of these two configurations would be better suited for a high-transaction database server, providing technical justifications for your choice.

Tutor's Breakdown

RAID 5 Architecture: Uses block-level striping with distributed parity. Data is split across all drives (minimum 3), and parity (reconstruction data) is written across all drives. If one drive fails, the array calculates the missing data using the parity blocks. It offers good read speeds and high storage efficiency but suffers from the "write penalty" (because parity must be calculated for every write operation).

RAID 10 Architecture: A nested RAID combining RAID 1 (Mirroring) and RAID 0 (Striping). Data is mirrored to a secondary set of drives, and then striped across those sets. It requires a minimum of 4 drives. It can survive multiple drive failures (as long as they aren't in the same mirror pair) and offers exceptional read and write speeds because there is no parity calculation.

Recommendation & Justification: For a high-transaction database server, RAID 10 is the superior choice.

  • Databases are I/O intensive, requiring fast, continuous read/write operations.
  • RAID 5 suffers from a significant write penalty due to parity calculation overhead, which creates a bottleneck in high-transaction database environments.
  • RAID 10 offers the fastest write performance while still maintaining complete fault tolerance via mirroring.
  • Rebuilding a failed drive in RAID 10 is much faster and less strenuous on the system than rebuilding a RAID 5 array (which requires reading all remaining disks to compute parity).
Review Storage Models

Question 3: Storage Models & HDD Mechanics (20 Marks)

Describe the internal physical components of a Hard Disk Drive (HDD) and explain the process by which data is read from and written to the platters. Furthermore, differentiate between the Direct Attached Storage (DAS), Network Attached Storage (NAS), and Storage Area Network (SAN) models, citing an ideal use-case for each.

Tutor's Breakdown

HDD Mechanics: An HDD consists of circular, magnetic platters connected to a rotating spindle. An actuator arm moves across the platters, holding read/write heads at its tip. To read/write data, the spindle spins the platters at high speeds (e.g., 7200 RPM). The actuator moves the head to the correct track and sector. Data is written by altering the magnetic polarization on the platter's surface (representing 1s and 0s) and read by detecting these magnetic changes.

Storage Models Differentiation:

  • DAS (Direct Attached Storage): Storage directly physically connected to a single server/workstation (e.g., internal hard drive, external USB drive). It is not networked.
    Use Case: A local workstation needing high-speed, isolated storage for video editing.
  • NAS (Network Attached Storage): A dedicated, file-level storage appliance connected to a standard Ethernet network. It acts as a file server, allowing multiple clients to access shared files over TCP/IP.
    Use Case: A small office needing a centralized, easily accessible location for employees to share Word documents and spreadsheets.
  • SAN (Storage Area Network): A high-performance, dedicated network (often using Fibre Channel or iSCSI) that provides block-level access to storage arrays. Servers see SAN storage as if it were a locally attached hard drive, rather than a network share.
    Use Case: An enterprise data center hosting massive, clustered databases or virtual machines that require maximum throughput, low latency, and block-level control.
Review SA Laws

Question 4: SA Favourite Laws & System Documentation (20 Marks)

System administration is guided by fundamental principles and rigorous record-keeping.
a) Identify and explain two well-known "System Administrator Favourite Laws" (e.g., Parkinson's Law as applied to storage). (10 marks)
b) Discuss why establishing and maintaining comprehensive system documentation is critical for disaster recovery and operational continuity. (10 marks)

Tutor's Breakdown

Part A: SA Laws

  • Parkinson's Law (as applied to storage): "Data expands to fill any void." This means that no matter how much storage capacity a System Administrator provisions, users and applications will eventually fill it. It highlights the futility of just buying more disks without implementing data management policies and quotas.
  • Murphy's Law: "Anything that can go wrong will go wrong." In system administration, this dictates a defensive posture. SAs must assume hardware will fail, software will crash, and human error will occur, necessitating robust backups, redundancy (like RAID), and failover systems.
  • (Alternative) Conway's Law: "Organizations which design systems are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of these organizations." Poor team communication leads to poorly integrated IT systems.

Part B: Documentation

  • Disaster Recovery (DR): During an outage, stress and panic are high. Relying on memory is dangerous. Comprehensive documentation provides a clear, step-by-step "runbook" for restoring services, rebuilding servers, and recovering data, drastically reducing downtime (MTTR).
  • Operational Continuity (Bus Factor): If a key System Administrator is unavailable (vacation, illness, leaving the company), undocumented systems become a major liability. Documentation ensures that knowledge is institutionalized rather than siloed in one person's head, allowing other team members to step in and manage the infrastructure without disruption.
Review SSH

Question 5: SSH and Remote Management (20 Marks)

Secure Shell (SSH) is a fundamental protocol for remote system management. Detail the step-by-step process of establishing a secure SSH connection utilizing public-key cryptography. In your response, explain how this method mitigates common security threats compared to traditional password-based authentication.

Tutor's Breakdown

Step-by-Step Process of Public-Key SSH:

  1. Key Generation: The client uses a tool (like ssh-keygen) to generate a cryptographic key pair: a private key (kept secret on the client) and a public key (safe to share).
  2. Key Distribution: The client's public key is copied to the remote server and appended to the user's ~/.ssh/authorized_keys file.
  3. Connection Initiation: The client initiates an SSH connection. The server verifies if the client's public key exists in its authorized list.
  4. Challenge-Response: If found, the server encrypts a random challenge message using the client's public key and sends it to the client.
  5. Decryption & Verification: The client uses its secret private key to decrypt the message, hashes it, and sends the hash back to the server. Because only the private key could decrypt the challenge, the server verifies the client's identity without any passwords being transmitted.

Security Advantages over Passwords:

  • Brute Force Mitigation: Passwords can be guessed or cracked via dictionary attacks. Cryptographic keys (e.g., RSA 2048-bit or Ed25519) are mathematically impossible to brute-force with current technology.
  • No Network Interception: Unlike passwords, the private key is never sent over the network. Even if an attacker intercepts the traffic, they cannot steal the credential.
  • Phishing Resistance: You cannot be socially engineered into "giving away" your private key in the same way you can be tricked into typing a password into a fake portal.
Review Unix Philosophy

Question 6: File Systems (Unix Philosophy & Principles) (20 Marks)

The Unix Philosophy heavily influences modern System Administration. Explain the concept of "Everything is a file" in Unix-like systems. Give two practical examples of how a system administrator can leverage this principle to monitor hardware or manipulate system processes using standard command-line tools.

Tutor's Breakdown

"Everything is a file" Concept: In Unix-like operating systems, the file system is the universal interface. Not only are text documents and directories treated as files, but so are hardware devices (hard drives, keyboards, printers), network connections (sockets), and even running processes. They are exposed to the user space through the Virtual File System (VFS) as nodes in the directory tree.

Practical Examples: Because they are treated as files, administrators can use standard, simple text-processing tools (like cat, echo, grep) to interact with them, rather than needing specialized APIs.

  • Hardware Monitoring via /dev or /sys: An administrator can read from hardware devices directly. For example, reading the contents of /proc/cpuinfo or /proc/meminfo using the cat command outputs real-time hardware specifications and memory usage in plain text. Sending output to a disk can be done directly by piping data into /dev/sda.
  • Process Manipulation via /proc: The /proc directory contains pseudo-files representing currently running processes (identified by their PID). An administrator can view the command line arguments used to start a specific process by reading /proc/[PID]/cmdline. They can also dynamically alter kernel parameters by echoing values into specific files, like echo 1 > /proc/sys/net/ipv4/ip_forward to enable IP forwarding instantly.

Question 7: System Administration Principles (Scalability & Automation) (20 Marks)

According to the "Principles of System Administration" documentation, Scalability, Security, and Simplicity form the three pillars of exceptional system design. Define "Scalability" in this context. Discuss why automation is a mandatory component of scalability, providing an example of a task that must be automated as a network grows from 10 servers to 1,000 servers.

Tutor's Breakdown

Definition of Scalability: In system administration, scalability refers to a system's (or a team's) ability to handle a growing amount of work gracefully, or its potential to be enlarged to accommodate that growth. It means the system's performance or the administrative overhead does not degrade disproportionately as demand (users, traffic, or number of machines) increases.

Why Automation is Mandatory for Scalability:

  • Human effort does not scale linearly. A System Administrator might easily manage 10 servers manually, but managing 1,000 servers manually would require an impossible 100-fold increase in working hours.
  • Manual processes are prone to human error, inconsistency ("configuration drift"), and fatigue. Automation ensures identical, predictable execution across any number of nodes.
  • Without automation, SAs spend all their time on "break-fix" reactive work rather than proactive design and engineering.

Example Task: Software Patching and Updates.

  • At 10 servers: An SA can SSH into each server individually, run `apt-get update && apt-get upgrade`, and manually verify the service restarted successfully. This might take an hour.
  • At 1,000 servers: Doing this manually is impossible. The task must be automated using configuration management tools (like Ansible, Puppet, or Chef) or bash scripting. The automated tool can push the updates in parallel, reboot systems in a staggered fashion to maintain uptime, and generate a centralized report of successes and failures.
